他的框架是 operator,不是 strategist。唯一 long term 能赢的就是持续开发新服务——每个季度,不是一次。他提醒创始人别掉进一个经典坑:以为可以空手走进一个复杂行业,把 incumbent 推翻之前,先搞清楚这门生意到底是什么。

放在 AI 时代,这条经验更尖锐。Priceline 的 agent 产品(Penny)过去几个月每月翻倍,但放在 $186B 的旅行盘子、10 亿+ room nights 面前,AI 在绝对量上仍然很小。规模把 moat 这件事讲得很具体。

没有 moat 这种东西。没有什么地方能让你被保护住、不被创新颠覆。今天你在某些领域有 competitive advantage,绝对有。但这些明天可能就消失。

长期能赢的唯一办法就是持续开发新服务、新做事方式。怎么把你的生意做得更好?你需要什么?哪里需要更多需求?你哪里在痛?

这生意复杂得多。如果你觉得你直接进来就能把这些大玩家推翻,我的建议是先搞清楚这门生意是什么,再决定要不要把资本押上去。

English

Booking Holdings CEO Glenn Fogel opens No Priors with a thesis the AI era is forcing every operator to face: there is no such thing as a moat. What you have today is a competitive advantage; tomorrow it can be gone.

His frame is operator, not strategist. The only durable move is to keep developing new services — every quarter, not once. The classic trap he warns founders against: assuming you can walk into a complex industry and displace incumbents without first understanding what the business actually is.

For an AI-era audience the lesson is sharper. Priceline's bet on an agent (Penny) doubled every month for the past few months, but in absolute terms AI is still tiny against $186B of travel and 1B+ room nights. Scale makes the moat discussion concrete.

There is no such thing as a moat. There is no such thing as somewhere you're gonna be protected against innovation. Today, we have a competitive advantage on areas. Absolutely. But those can go away tomorrow.

The only one to win long term is to continue to develop new services, new ways to do things. How can we do your business better? What do you need? Where you need more demand? Where are you hurting?

It's so much more complex. Complex. If you think you're just gonna come in and do this business and knock away these very big players, I'd say you should really understand what the business is before you decide to commit your capital.

中文

Box CEO Aaron Levie 把这条反直觉的数字摆出来了:软件相关 job postings 的增速超过其他领域,跟「AI 取代程序员」那个叙事完全反过来。原因——如果你把某样有很多 use case 的东西的生产成本压下去,人们想要的是更多,不是更少。

重点不是 AI 不改变工作(它确实改变),重点是需求曲线比供给曲线先往上抬,至少在第一个 product cycle 是这样。会被要的软件变多,会雇来 ship 软件的人也变多,产能会冒到没人计划过的地方。

Fogel 的「no moat」和 Levie 的就业数据其实是同一个论点的两个面:incumbent 的优势是被一段段磨掉的,不是被一次性击穿的。moat 不是墙,是一条被 AI 不断往上抬的移动成本曲线。

那个 AI 据说要取代的岗位,正在经历预期之外的结果。软件相关 job postings 的增速超过其他领域。

为什么?如果你把某样有大量 use case 的东西的生产成本压下去,人们想要的是更多,不是更少。

这是关于 AI 对就业影响里更细的那一面。需求曲线比供给曲线先往上抬。

English

Aaron Levie (Box CEO) is publishing the counter-intuitive number now: software job postings are outpacing other fields — the opposite of the 'AI replaces programmers' story. If you lower the cost of producing something with lots of use cases, people want more of it, not less.

The point isn't that AI doesn't change work — it does. The point is that the demand curve bends up faster than supply shifts, at least for the first product cycle. More software gets asked for, more people get hired to ship it, more capacity shows up in places nobody planned for.

Fogel's 'no moat' thesis and Levie's jobs data are the same argument from two angles: incumbent advantage erodes in steps, not in one blow. The moat isn't a wall — it's a moving cost curve that AI keeps bending.

The job that AI was supposed to replace is experiencing the opposite of the expected outcome. Software job postings are outpacing other fields.

Why is that? If you lower the cost of production of something that has lots of use cases, people want more of it, not less.

This is the more nuanced take on what AI does to jobs. The demand curve bends up faster than the supply curve shifts.

English

Swyx extends Jevons Paradox out of coding into all knowledge work — the same mechanism that exploded software demand in the agentic-engineering era is now visible wherever humans can wield coding-style agents.

Three conditions that make the Jevons move hit harder: humans who are good at wielding coding agents, coding agents themselves breaking containment into adjacent knowledge work, and a unit-cost collapse on labor that lifts total demand rather than lowering it.

His punchline: what happened to coding isn't the exception, it's the herald. If you only internalized Jevons as a software story, you missed the version that's about to hit every other knowledge-work domain.
